# Part 1: Gold Fundamentals `[ALL]`

## 1.1 Why Gold Matters

Gold (chemical symbol: Au, from Latin *aurum*) has been valued by humans for over 5,000 years. Understanding its basic physical properties is essential because they directly determine how gold deposits form, how we find gold, and how we extract it.

### Key Physical Properties

| Property | Value | Why It Matters |
|----------|-------|----------------|
| Density | 19.3 g/cm³ | ~7x heavier than rock. This is why gold sinks to the bottom of rivers and why gravity separation works |
| Chemical Inertness | Does not rust, corrode, or dissolve in most acids | Gold survives millions of years of weathering. The original rock disappears but the gold remains |
| Malleability | Most malleable metal | Gold deforms rather than breaks during river transport — flattened into flakes and nuggets |
| Melting Point | 1,064°C | Achievable with basic equipment, enabling on-site smelting |
| Crustal Abundance | 0.004 ppm (4 parts per billion) | Extremely rare. Even a 1 g/t deposit represents 250x natural concentration |

**Practical implications:**
- Gold's extreme density is the foundation of alluvial mining — gravity methods work because gold is so much heavier than everything else
- Gold's chemical inertness means it survives weathering and accumulates in rivers over millions of years, creating alluvial deposits
- A "mineable" gold deposit requires geological processes to concentrate gold hundreds to thousands of times above its natural background level

## 1.2 How Gold Is Measured

### Grade

Grade is the concentration of gold in rock or sediment. It is the single most important number in gold mining.

| Unit | Meaning | Context |
|------|---------|---------|
| g/t (grams per tonne) | Grams of gold per tonne of rock | Standard for hard rock mining. 1 g/t = 1 ppm |
| g/m³ (grams per cubic metre) | Grams of gold per cubic metre of gravel | Standard for alluvial mining |
| oz/t (ounces per short ton) | Troy ounces per short ton | Used in older North American reports. 1 oz/t = 34.29 g/t |

**Grade benchmarks (hard rock):**
- Earth's average crustal gold: 0.004 g/t (background)
- Low grade: 0.5–1.0 g/t
- Medium grade: 1.0–5.0 g/t
- High grade: 5.0–20 g/t
- Bonanza grade: >20 g/t

**Grade benchmarks (alluvial):**
- Sub-economic: <0.1 g/m³
- Low grade: 0.1–0.3 g/m³
- Medium grade: 0.3–0.5 g/m³
- Good grade: 0.5–1.0 g/m³
- High grade: >1.0 g/m³
- Very high grade: >2.0 g/m³

### Key Units

| Conversion | Value |
|------------|-------|
| 1 troy ounce (oz) | 31.1035 grams |
| 1 metric tonne | 1,000 kilograms |
| 1 kilogram of gold | 32.15 troy ounces |
| 1 short ton | 907.185 kilograms |

### Purity (Fineness)

Gold purity is expressed in fineness (parts per thousand) or karats:
- 999.9 fine = 99.99% pure = 24 karat = "four nines" (investment grade)
- 995 fine = 99.5% pure = LBMA Good Delivery minimum
- 900 fine = 90% pure = typical doré bar from alluvial operations
- 750 fine = 75% pure = 18 karat (jewellery)

# Part 2: Types of Gold Deposits `[ALL]`

## 2.1 Two Worlds: Hard Rock vs Alluvial

All gold deposits fall into two broad categories:

**Primary (Hard Rock / Lode) Deposits** — Gold locked inside rock, placed there by geological processes millions of years ago. Must be drilled, blasted, crushed, and chemically processed to extract.

**Secondary (Alluvial / Placer) Deposits** — Gold that has been freed from rock by natural weathering and concentrated in rivers and sediments by gravity. Can be extracted using simple gravity methods.

## 2.2 Major Hard Rock Deposit Types

| Type | How It Forms | Typical Grade | Typical Size | Key Examples |
|------|-------------|---------------|-------------|--------------|
| **Orogenic** | Hot fluids migrate along deep fault zones during mountain-building events, depositing gold in quartz veins | 1–20 g/t | <0.5 to >50 Moz | Obuasi (Ghana), Loulo-Gounkoto (Mali), Kalgoorlie (Australia) |
| **Epithermal** | Near-surface (<1.5 km) deposits linked to volcanic activity | 1–15 g/t | 0.5–10 Moz | Hishikari (Japan), Yanacocha (Peru) |
| **Porphyry** | Massive low-grade deposits around cooling magma bodies at subduction zones | 0.3–1.5 g/t | 5–100+ Moz | Grasberg (Indonesia), Oyu Tolgoi (Mongolia) |
| **Carlin-type** | Ultra-fine "invisible" gold trapped in pyrite and arsenopyrite in carbonate rocks | 1–10 g/t | 1–30 Moz | Carlin Trend (Nevada, USA) |
| **VMS** | Formed at underwater volcanic centres; gold is a by-product of copper-zinc deposits | Variable | Variable | Horne (Canada) |

**The most important type in West Africa is Orogenic — this is what makes West Africa one of the world's premier gold regions.**

## 2.3 Alluvial (Placer) Gold Deposits

Alluvial gold forms when primary deposits are weathered and eroded. Gold particles are freed from the rock and transported by water. Because gold is 7x heavier than the surrounding sand and gravel, it sinks to the bottom, naturally concentrating at specific points along rivers and streams.

### Where Gold Concentrates in a River

- **Bedrock surface**: Gold sinks through gravel until it hits solid rock. The bottom 0.5–2.0 m of gravel and the top 0.3–1.0 m of weathered bedrock is typically the "pay zone"
- **Bedrock cracks and crevices**: Gold gets trapped in irregularities — cleaning bedrock thoroughly is critical
- **Inside of river bends**: Water slows on the inner bank, dropping heavy particles. These "point bars" are classic alluvial targets
- **Behind obstacles**: Large boulders create low-velocity zones where gold settles
- **Where gradient changes**: Where a steep stream enters a flat valley, gold drops out as water speed decreases (alluvial fans)

### Types of Secondary Deposits

| Type | Distance from Source | Gold Shape | Notes |
|------|---------------------|------------|-------|
| Eluvial (residual) | 0–50 m | Angular, rough | Gold hasn't travelled far — the primary source is directly uphill |
| Colluvial (slope) | 50–500 m | Sub-angular | Transitional |
| Alluvial (river) | 500 m to tens of km | Rounded, flattened | Classic placer deposits. Often accompanied by "black sand" (magnetite, ilmenite) |

**Note on alluvial mining:** Alluvial mining has much lower cut-off grades than hard rock because there is no blasting, crushing, or chemical processing needed. The gold is already "free gold" that can be recovered by simple gravity methods.

## 2.4 West Africa Gold Geology

### Why West Africa Is So Gold-Rich

West Africa sits on the **West African Craton** — one of Earth's oldest and most stable continental cores (>2.0 billion years old). The southern part, called the **Leo-Man Shield**, hosts the **Birimian greenstone belts** — a series of ancient volcanic and sedimentary rock belts that are among the most gold-endowed geological formations on Earth.

The key event was the **Eburnean Orogeny** (~2.15–2.07 billion years ago) — a massive continental collision that:
1. Folded and faulted the Birimian rocks
2. Created deep crustal-scale shear zones (major fault systems)
3. Generated enormous volumes of hot, gold-bearing fluids
4. These fluids migrated up along the shear zones and deposited gold — creating the orogenic gold deposits we see today

### Major Gold Belts in West Africa

| Belt | Location | Key Deposits | Significance |
|------|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Ashanti Belt** | Ghana (NE-SW, ~200 km) | Obuasi (>30 Moz), Tarkwa | Reference for orogenic gold |
| **Senegalo-Malian Shear Zone** | Mali–Senegal (NNE-SSW, >500 km) | Loulo-Gounkoto (~15+ Moz), Fekola (~10+ Moz) | **Major producing region** |
| **Siguiri Basin** | Guinea (NE) | Siguiri (>10 Moz) | Significant gold province |
| **Ity-Bondoukou Belt** | Côte d'Ivoire (western) | Ity (>5 Moz), Yaouré (~3 Moz) | Active exploration region |
| **Burkina Faso Belts** | Burkina Faso | Essakane (>10 Moz), Houndé | Regional context |

### Key Reference: Loulo-Gounkoto (Mali)

This is one of the most important reference mines in West Africa:
- **Owner**: Barrick Gold (80%), Mali Government (20%)
- **Geology**: Orogenic gold in the Senegalo-Malian Shear Zone, hosted in Birimian greywacke-phyllite-metavolcanic sequence
- **Resource**: >15 Moz, underground grades 3–6 g/t, open pit 1.5–3 g/t
- **Key insight**: Deep oxidation (laterite/saprolite extends 40–80 m) means the upper portion contains free gold that can be processed with simple gravity + CIL methods, while deeper fresh rock requires more complex flotation processing

# Part 3: Exploration — From Idea to Drill Target `[ALL]` `[BD]` `[MGMT]`

## 3.1 The Exploration Pipeline

Gold exploration follows a systematic funnel: start wide, narrow down progressively. At each stage, you spend more money but reduce uncertainty. Most prospects fail — the industry average success rate from grassroots to economic discovery is roughly **1 in 500 to 1 in 1,000**.

### Stage-by-Stage Overview

| Stage | What You Do | Typical Cost | Timeline | Key Deliverable | Success Rate |
|-------|------------|-------------|----------|-----------------|--------------|
| **1. Desktop Study** | Review existing geological maps, satellite imagery, historical reports, published data | $50K–$500K | 1–6 months | Target areas identified | ~1 in 10 become field targets |
| **2. Remote Sensing** | Satellite multispectral/hyperspectral analysis, airborne magnetics, regional gravity | $100K–$2M | 3–12 months | Structural and lithological maps, anomaly targets | — |
| **3. Geological Mapping** | Field geologists walk the ground, record rock types, structures, alteration, mineralization | $200K–$1M | 2–6 months | Detailed geological maps | — |
| **4. Geochemistry** | Stream sediment sampling, soil sampling (grid at 100–200 m line spacing), rock chip sampling | $500K–$5M | 6–18 months | Gold-in-soil anomalies, target ranking | ~1 in 5 soil anomalies test positive by drilling |
| **5. Geophysics (Ground)** | Induced Polarization (IP), Electromagnetics (EM) — detect sulfide mineralization through soil cover | $500K–$3M | 3–12 months | Drill targets refined | — |
| **6. Initial Drilling** | Wide-spaced (100–200 m) Reverse Circulation (RC) drilling to test anomalies | $2M–$10M | 6–18 months | Discovery confirmation or target elimination | ~1 in 10 drill programs find economic mineralization |
| **7. Resource Drilling** | Infill RC + Diamond Drilling (DD) at 25–50 m spacing, 3D geological modelling | $10M–$50M | 1–3 years | Mineral Resource Estimate (JORC/NI 43-101) | — |
| **8. Feasibility Studies** | PEA → PFS → DFS/BFS — increasing levels of engineering and economic study | $2M–$30M | 1–3 years | Technical and economic viability confirmed | — |

### Stage 1: Desktop Study (Literature Review & Target Generation)

The desktop study is where every exploration program begins — and where the best geologists earn their keep before spending a dollar in the field. The goal is to compile and analyze all existing information about a region to identify the most promising areas for follow-up.

**What it involves:**
- Reviewing published geological maps (typically 1:200,000 or 1:100,000 scale from national geological surveys)
- Compiling historical exploration reports — previous companies may have drilled, sampled, or mapped the area. Their data is often publicly available (or purchasable) and can save millions
- Analyzing academic research papers on regional geology, structural controls, and metallogeny
- Reviewing government databases for known mineral occurrences, artisanal mining sites, and past production records
- Downloading freely available satellite imagery (Landsat, Sentinel-2, ASTER) for preliminary interpretation

**Why it matters:** A thorough desktop study can eliminate 90% of an area from consideration before any fieldwork begins. The best exploration geologists find deposits that others missed — often because they read reports that were sitting in a government archive for decades. This stage is extremely high-ROI: $50K–$500K spent here can save $5M+ in wasted field programs.

**Key deliverables:** Target area map, compilation database, summary report ranking targets by priority.

---

### Stage 2: Remote Sensing & Airborne Surveys

Remote sensing bridges the gap between desktop research and boots-on-the-ground fieldwork. It uses satellite and airborne instruments to detect geological features and mineral signatures invisible to the naked eye.

**What it involves:**
- **Multispectral/Hyperspectral satellite analysis:** Processing satellite data (ASTER, WorldView-3, Sentinel-2) to map alteration minerals associated with gold — iron oxides (gossans), clay minerals (sericite, kaolinite), carbonates. These minerals form "alteration halos" around gold-bearing structures
- **Airborne magnetics:** Flying a magnetometer on a plane or helicopter at 50–80 m above ground to map magnetic minerals in the subsurface. In West Africa, this is critical because thick laterite cover (10–60 m) hides the underlying geology
- **Airborne radiometrics:** Measures natural radioactivity (K, U, Th) from rocks — helps map different rock types and identify potassic alteration associated with some gold systems
- **Digital Elevation Models (DEMs):** Extracting structural lineaments (faults, shear zones) from topographic data — gold deposits in West Africa are overwhelmingly controlled by structures

**Why it matters:** Airborne magnetic surveys are arguably the single most important geophysical tool in West African gold exploration. The Birimian greenstone belts that host 95%+ of West Africa's gold are strongly magnetic, and structures that control gold mineralization create clear magnetic signatures. A regional aeromagnetic survey can define drill targets that would take years to find through surface methods alone.

**Key deliverables:** Structural interpretation maps, alteration anomaly maps, prioritized target list for field follow-up.

---

### Stage 3: Geological Mapping (Field Reconnaissance)

This is when geologists finally walk the ground. Geological mapping is the fundamental skill of exploration — everything else builds on it. The geologist's job is to observe, record, and interpret what the rocks are telling them.

**What it involves:**
- Walking systematic traverses across the target area (typically at 200–400 m spacing for regional mapping, 50–100 m for detailed)
- Recording rock types (lithology), structural features (bedding, foliation, faults, shear zones, quartz veins), and alteration (color changes, new minerals)
- Collecting rock samples for thin section petrography and geochemical analysis
- Mapping artisanal mining sites — in West Africa, local miners are often the best pathfinders. If artisanal miners are digging in an area, there is gold. The question is how much and at what grade
- Recording GPS locations, photographs, and field notes that will be compiled into a geological map

**Why it matters:** No amount of remote sensing or geophysics can replace a good geologist on the ground. Mapping reveals the 3D geometry of the geology — how rocks are folded, faulted, and intruded — which controls where gold is deposited. A well-mapped project also builds credibility with investors and joint venture partners.

**Challenges in West Africa:** Deep laterite weathering means rock outcrop is rare (sometimes <5% of the surface). Geologists must rely on road cuts, river beds, artisanal pits, and termite mounds (which bring subsurface material to surface) to see the geology.

**Key deliverables:** Geological map (1:10,000 to 1:50,000 scale), structural interpretation, sample locations, field report with target recommendations.

---

### Stage 4: Geochemistry (Soil, Stream & Rock Sampling)

Geochemistry is the process of systematically collecting and analyzing earth materials to find chemical signatures of buried gold mineralization. It is the workhorse method for defining drill targets in West Africa.

**What it involves:**
- **Stream sediment sampling:** Collecting fine-grained sediment (<200 mesh / 75 μm) from active stream beds. One sample integrates the geochemistry of the entire upstream catchment. Gold and pathfinder elements (As, Sb, W, Bi) above background indicate a source somewhere upstream
- **Soil sampling:** The most important surface exploration method in gold exploration. Samples are collected from the B or C soil horizon (typically 30–80 cm depth) on a systematic grid. Typical spacing is 100–200 m between lines and 25–50 m between sample points
- **Rock chip sampling:** Collecting rock fragments from outcrops, float, or artisanal mine workings. Provides direct evidence of gold grades but is limited by exposure
- **Termite mound sampling:** Unique to tropical Africa — termites bring material from 10–40 m depth to surface, providing a "drill hole" through the laterite cap

**Why it matters:** A well-executed soil sampling program can define gold anomalies that directly correspond to underlying mineralization — even through 20–40 m of barren laterite cover. The key is understanding what you're sampling: in lateritic terrain, gold is often concentrated in specific soil horizons, and sampling the wrong horizon gives false negatives.

**Cost context:** A regional stream sediment survey (500–1,000 samples at $30–50/sample including collection) costs $30K–$80K and can cover hundreds of km². A detailed soil sampling grid (5,000–20,000 samples) over a priority target costs $300K–$2M. This is one of the highest-value expenditures in exploration.

**Key deliverables:** Geochemical anomaly maps, gold-in-soil contour plots, target ranking for drill testing.

---

### Stage 5: Ground Geophysics

Ground geophysics provides higher-resolution subsurface information than airborne surveys, helping to precisely locate and define drill targets. At this stage, you're spending real money and need confidence in your targets.

**What it involves:**
- **Induced Polarization (IP):** Injects electrical current into the ground and measures how rocks store and release charge. Sulfide minerals (pyrite, arsenopyrite) — which are intimately associated with gold in orogenic deposits — produce strong IP anomalies ("chargeability" highs). IP is the most widely used ground geophysical method in gold exploration
- **Electromagnetic (EM) surveys:** Ground EM (moving loop, fixed loop) maps conductive features — graphitic shear zones, sulfide-rich zones, conductive structures. Useful for detecting mineralized structures beneath thick cover
- **Gravity surveys:** Measures subtle variations in gravitational acceleration caused by density contrasts in rocks. Can detect quartz-filled structures, lithological contacts, and faults
- **Ground magnetics:** Higher-resolution version of airborne magnetics, used to map specific structures at target scale

**Why it matters:** IP surveys are particularly powerful in West Africa because orogenic gold deposits are almost always associated with sulfide minerals. A coincident gold-in-soil anomaly + IP chargeability high + structural target from magnetics = a high-priority drill target. This multi-dataset approach dramatically improves drill success rates.

**Typical survey design:** IP lines are surveyed at 100–200 m spacing with dipole-dipole or pole-dipole arrays. A single IP line costs $3K–$8K depending on terrain and line length. A typical ground IP program covers 50–200 line-km.

**Key deliverables:** IP chargeability and resistivity sections, integrated target maps combining geology + geochemistry + geophysics, drill hole location plans.

---

### Stage 6: Initial Drilling (Discovery Drilling)

This is the moment of truth. After all the desktop analysis, remote sensing, mapping, sampling, and geophysical surveys, you finally put steel in the ground to test whether there is gold at economic grades and widths.

**What it involves:**
- **Reverse Circulation (RC) drilling** at wide spacing (typically 100–200 m between holes, sometimes 400 m for first-pass testing)
- Holes are typically 100–200 m deep in West Africa (deeper if targeting known structures at depth)
- Samples are collected every 1 m and submitted for fire assay
- Geological logging of RC chips to build a subsurface geological model
- Typically 20–60 holes in an initial program, testing 3–8 targets

**Why it matters:** This is where the vast majority of exploration programs end. Statistically, only about 1 in 10 initial drilling programs find mineralization worth following up, and only about 1 in 100 grassroots targets becomes an economic deposit. A "discovery hole" — one that intersects significant gold mineralization over economic widths — can transform a project overnight. A single result like "48m @ 3.2 g/t gold from 52m depth" can multiply a project's value 10x.

**What a good result looks like:**
- **Grade × width** is what matters. "2m @ 50 g/t" (narrow, high-grade) and "100m @ 1 g/t" (wide, lower-grade) are both interesting but represent very different deposit types
- Consistent mineralization over meaningful widths (>5m for open-pittable, >2m for underground at high grades)
- Multiple holes intersecting mineralization — a single hole is never enough

**What kills a target:** Consistently sub-economic grades (<0.5 g/t over narrow widths), erratic gold distribution with no geological continuity, or metallurgical issues (refractory gold that doesn't respond to standard processing).

**Key deliverables:** Drill results (significant intercepts), cross-sections, geological model, decision to advance or drop the target.

### Stage 7: Resource Drilling (Infill & Definition Drilling)

If initial drilling confirms a discovery, the next step is to drill enough holes to define the size, shape, and grade of the deposit so that a Mineral Resource Estimate can be prepared.

**What it involves:**
- **Infill RC drilling** at 50–100 m spacing to improve geological confidence
- **Diamond Drilling (DD)** at 25–50 m spacing for high-confidence resource categories. DD produces continuous rock core that provides:
  - Detailed geological, structural, and alteration information
  - Oriented core for structural measurements
  - Metallurgical test samples (you need core to determine how to process the ore)
  - Geotechnical data for mine design (rock strength, fracturing)
- **3D geological modelling** using software (Leapfrog, Micromine, Datamine) to build wireframes of ore zones
- **Geostatistical analysis** (variography, kriging) to estimate grade distribution

**Why it matters:** The Mineral Resource Estimate (MRE) is the single most important document in the life of a mining project. It classifies the deposit into:
- **Inferred Resource:** Low geological confidence (wide-spaced drilling). Good enough to demonstrate potential but not to base investment decisions on
- **Indicated Resource:** Moderate confidence (closer-spaced drilling). Sufficient for a Pre-Feasibility Study
- **Measured Resource:** High confidence (close-spaced drilling). Required for a Definitive Feasibility Study and mine permitting

The MRE must be prepared by a Competent Person (CP) under the JORC Code or a Qualified Person (QP) under NI 43-101. The CP/QP takes personal legal responsibility for the estimate.

**Cost context:** Resource drilling is the most capital-intensive exploration stage. A typical program involves 30,000–100,000 m of combined RC + DD drilling at $30–200/m, plus geological modelling, metallurgical testing, and the resource estimate itself.

**Key deliverables:** JORC/NI 43-101 Mineral Resource Estimate, 3D geological model, metallurgical test results, cross-sections and long-sections showing ore zone geometry.

### Stage 8: Feasibility Studies (PEA → PFS → DFS)

Feasibility studies are the engineering and economic analyses that determine whether a mineral resource can be profitably mined. They progress through three levels of increasing detail and cost.

**What it involves:**

**Preliminary Economic Assessment (PEA):**
- First economic "sanity check" — can this deposit make money?
- Order-of-magnitude engineering: conceptual mine plan, generic processing flowsheet, rough capital and operating cost estimates (±35–50% accuracy)
- Typically costs $500K–$2M
- Can be based on Inferred Resources (unlike PFS/DFS)

**Pre-Feasibility Study (PFS):**
- More detailed engineering study (±25% cost accuracy)
- Mine design, processing plant design, infrastructure layout, environmental baseline
- Must be based on at least Indicated Resources
- Typically costs $3M–$10M
- Banks and major investors typically require at least a PFS before committing capital

**Definitive Feasibility Study (DFS / Bankable Feasibility Study / BFS):**
- Full engineering study (±15% cost accuracy) — the basis for a mine construction decision
- Detailed mine plan, process plant engineering, infrastructure design, environmental and social impact assessments
- Must be based on Measured + Indicated Resources
- Includes pilot plant metallurgical testing, geotechnical studies, hydrology, detailed CAPEX/OPEX estimation
- Typically costs $10M–$30M+
- This is the document that banks use to approve project finance (hence "bankable")

**Key economic metrics assessed:**
- **NPV (Net Present Value):** The discounted present value of future cash flows, minus capital costs. Positive NPV = the project creates value
- **IRR (Internal Rate of Return):** The discount rate at which NPV = 0. Most gold projects need >15% IRR to attract investment, >20% to be considered strong
- **AISC (All-In Sustaining Cost):** Total cost per ounce of gold produced, including mining, processing, G&A, sustaining capital, royalties. Currently, the industry average is ~$1,300–$1,500/oz
- **Payback period:** Time to recover initial capital investment. Banks prefer <5 years

**Key deliverables:** Technical report (JORC/NI 43-101 compliant), financial model, mine plan, environmental and social impact assessment, risk register, recommendation to proceed (or not) to construction.

### Drilling Methods

| Method | Depth | Sample Quality | Cost | Primary Use |
|--------|-------|----------------|------|-------------|
| RAB (Rotary Air Blast) | 10–50 m | Low | $10–20/m | Reconnaissance, penetrating laterite cover |
| **RC (Reverse Circulation)** | 50–300 m | Good | $30–80/m | **Main workhorse for West Africa gold exploration** |
| DD (Diamond Drilling) | >2,000 m | Highest (core) | $80–200/m | Detailed geology, metallurgical samples, deep targets |

### Typical West Africa Drilling Sequence
1. RAB/auger holes to test through deep laterite cover
2. Wide-spaced RC (100–200 m) to test anomaly extent
3. Infill RC (50–100 m) to define mineralization geometry
4. DD core drilling for detailed geology and metallurgical testing
5. Close-spaced DD (25–50 m) for Measured Resource classification

### Assaying (Laboratory Analysis)

**Fire Assay** is the gold standard for gold analysis, used for over 500 years:
1. Sample preparation: crush 2–3 kg to <2 mm, split and pulverize to >90% passing 75 μm
2. Fusion: 30–50 g sample mixed with flux (PbO, Na₂CO₃, borax), melted at 1,100°C
3. Cupellation: lead button is oxidized at 950°C, leaving a gold-silver bead (doré bead)
4. Measurement: weighed (gravimetric) or dissolved and analysed by AAS/ICP

**Quality Control (QA/QC) is critical:**
- Blanks: check for contamination
- Certified Reference Materials (CRMs): check analytical accuracy
- Duplicates: check reproducibility
- Umpire assays: 5–10% of samples sent to a second laboratory

### The "Nugget Effect"

Gold is distributed extremely unevenly in deposits. A small sample may completely miss gold particles, or contain one large particle that inflates the result. This is the biggest challenge in estimating alluvial gold grades. The solution: use **large-volume samples** (bulk sampling).

## 3.2 Exploration Geochemistry `[OPS]`

### Stream Sediment Sampling
- Collect fine-grained sediment (-80 mesh) from active stream beds
- Most effective regional method — one sample "drains" a large catchment area
- Gold anomalies indicate upstream source

### Soil Sampling
- Collect samples from the B or C soil horizon on a systematic grid
- Typical spacing: 100–200 m between lines, 25–50 m between samples
- **The most important surface method for gold exploration in West Africa**
- Results are contoured to produce gold-in-soil anomaly maps

### Rock Chip Sampling
- Collect rock fragments from outcrops
- Confirms gold mineralization exists and provides approximate grades
- Limited by laterite cover in West Africa (outcrops are rare)

## 3.3 Exploration Geophysics `[OPS]`

### Airborne Magnetics
- Measures variations in the magnetic field caused by magnetic minerals (magnetite, etc.)
- **Extremely effective in West Africa** — can "see" through thick laterite cover
- Maps greenstone belt geometry, intrusive contacts, and structures
- Usually the first geophysical survey conducted

### Induced Polarization (IP)
- Injects electric current into the ground and measures the "chargeability" of rocks
- Sulfide minerals (pyrite, arsenopyrite) produce strong IP responses
- High chargeability + low resistivity = likely sulfide-rich altered rock = potential gold target

### Electromagnetic Methods (EM)
- Airborne EM (VTEM, ZTEM) and ground EM
- Maps conductive layers: graphitic shales, sulfide-rich zones, structures
- Useful for identifying shear zones beneath cover

## 3.4 Remote Sensing & Satellite Technology `[MGMT]` `[BD]`

Remote sensing is one of the most powerful and cost-effective tools in modern gold exploration. It allows geologists to analyze vast areas from space before ever setting foot on the ground.

### Satellite Platforms Used in Exploration

| Platform | Resolution | Key Capabilities | Cost |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|------|
| **Landsat 8/9** (NASA) | 30 m multispectral, 15 m pan | Iron oxide mapping, vegetation anomalies, regional geology | Free |
| **Sentinel-2** (ESA) | 10–20 m multispectral | Similar to Landsat but higher resolution; lithological mapping | Free |
| **ASTER** (NASA/Japan) | 15–90 m, 14 bands | Best free platform for alteration mineral mapping (clays, iron oxides, carbonates) | Free |

### Key Remote Sensing Techniques for Gold Exploration

**1. Spectral Analysis — Mapping Alteration Minerals**

Gold deposits create "alteration halos" — zones of chemically altered rock around the mineralization. These alteration minerals have distinct spectral signatures detectable from space:

| Alteration Type | Minerals | Spectral Band | Significance |
|----------------|----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Iron oxide** | Hematite, goethite, jarosite | VNIR (0.4–1.0 μm) | Indicates weathered sulfide zones; "gossans" are classic surface indicators of underlying mineralization |
| **Hydroxyl (clay)** | Sericite, kaolinite, illite | SWIR (2.0–2.5 μm) | Hydrothermal alteration — the "plumbing system" that carried gold-bearing fluids |
| **Carbonate** | Calcite, dolomite, ankerite | SWIR (2.3–2.5 μm) | Carbonate alteration associated with orogenic gold |
| **Silicification** | Quartz flooding | TIR (8–12 μm) | Direct host of gold in many orogenic deposits |

**2. Structural Analysis — Finding the Plumbing**

Gold deposits in West Africa are controlled by major crustal structures (shear zones, faults). Remote sensing maps these structures through:
- **Lineament extraction** from digital elevation models (SRTM, ALOS)
- **Magnetic data** (airborne or satellite-derived) to map deep crustal structures beneath laterite cover
- **Radar imagery** (SAR) that penetrates vegetation canopy — critical in tropical West Africa where forest cover hides outcrop

**3. Multi-Layer Integration (GIS Overlay)**

The most powerful approach combines multiple data layers in a Geographic Information System (GIS):

```
Layer 1: Geological map (rock types, known deposits)
Layer 2: Structural map (faults, shear zones from DEM + magnetics)
Layer 3: Alteration map (iron oxide + clay anomalies from satellite)
Layer 4: Geochemical data (stream sediment, soil sampling results)
Layer 5: Geophysical data (magnetics, IP, gravity)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Output: Ranked target areas where multiple layers converge
```

**The "four-layer overlay" (四图叠合)** is a powerful integrated method — combining geological mapping, structural interpretation, satellite alteration analysis, and geochemical data to generate drill targets.

# Part 4: Resource Estimation & Reporting Standards `[ALL]` `[LEGAL]` `[FINANCE]`

## 4.1 Resources vs Reserves

This is one of the most important concepts in mining. The distinction has legal, financial, and technical implications.

### Mineral Resources

A **Mineral Resource** is a concentration of minerals in the earth that has reasonable prospects for economic extraction. Resources are classified by confidence level:

| Category | Confidence | Data Requirements | Can Be Used For |
|----------|------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Inferred** | Low — geologically reasonable but uncertain | Limited data, wide drill spacing | Preliminary Economic Assessment (PEA) only. **Cannot be used in feasibility studies** |
| **Indicated** | Moderate — reasonable assumption of continuity | Sufficient data for geological continuity | Pre-Feasibility Study (PFS) |
| **Measured** | High — known with high confidence | Dense data, close drill spacing | Feasibility Study (FS/BFS) |

### Mineral Reserves

A **Mineral Reserve** is the economically mineable part of a Measured or Indicated Resource, demonstrated by at least a PFS including consideration of mining, metallurgical, economic, marketing, legal, environmental, social, and governmental factors.

| Category | Derived From | Confidence |
|----------|-------------|------------|
| **Probable Reserve** | Indicated Resource | Moderate |
| **Proven Reserve** | Measured Resource | High |

**Key principle**: Resources are not Reserves. Many published Resources will never become Reserves because they fail economic, environmental, or other tests.

## 4.2 Reporting Standards `[LEGAL]`

All public mineral resource and reserve disclosures must comply with an internationally recognized reporting standard and be signed by a qualified professional.

| Standard | Jurisdiction | Exchange | Qualified Professional |
|----------|-------------|----------|----------------------|
| **JORC** (Joint Ore Reserves Committee) | Australia | ASX | Competent Person (CP) — AusIMM or similar |
| **NI 43-101** | Canada | TSX, TSX-V | Qualified Person (QP) — P.Geo., P.Eng. |
| **S-K 1300** | USA | NYSE, NASDAQ | Qualified Person |
| **CIM** | Canada | — | Part of NI 43-101 framework |

**Legal implications:**
- You **cannot** publicly claim to have a "resource" or "reserve" without a report signed by a CP/QP under the relevant standard
- Using resource/reserve terminology without qualification in investor communications can constitute securities fraud
- "Inferred Resources" carry explicit warnings and cannot be converted to reserves
- A Competent Person must have at least 5 years' relevant experience and be a member of a recognized professional body

## 4.3 The Feasibility Ladder

| Study | Accuracy | Based On | Purpose | Typical Cost |
|-------|----------|----------|---------|-------------|
| **Scoping Study / PEA** (Preliminary Economic Assessment) | ±35–50% | Inferred + Indicated Resources | Is this project worth further investigation? | $0.5–2M |
| **PFS** (Pre-Feasibility Study) | ±25–30% | Indicated Resources (minimum) | Is this project likely economic? Begin permitting | $2–10M |
| **DFS / BFS** (Definitive / Bankable Feasibility Study) | ±10–15% | Measured + Indicated Resources | Final investment decision. Required by banks for project finance | $5–30M |

**Note:** Most junior exploration companies spend the majority of their time in the exploration and target-testing phase. Resource estimation and feasibility studies become relevant once drilling confirms economic mineralization.

# Part 5: Mining & Processing Methods `[ALL]` `[OPS]`

## 5.1 Open Pit Mining

The most common mining method in West Africa, especially for near-surface deposits with thick oxidized zones.

### Key Design Parameters

| Parameter | Description | Typical Values |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Overall Slope Angle** | Angle from pit bottom to rim | Laterite: 35–45°, Fresh rock: 45–60° |
| **Bench Height** | Height of each step in the pit | Small mines: 5–6 m, Large: 10–15 m |
| **Strip Ratio** | Tonnes of waste per tonne of ore | Economic range: 3:1 to 10:1 (higher ratios viable at current gold prices) |
| **Minimum Pit Bottom Width** | Must fit equipment safely | 30–40 m minimum |

### Daily Operations Cycle
1. **Drilling**: Drill blast holes on a grid pattern (2.5–4.0 m spacing)
2. **Blasting**: Licensed blaster uses ANFO or emulsion explosives. Clear area, sound warning, blast, wait for dust to settle (30–60 min), check for misfires
3. **Loading**: Excavator loads rock into haul trucks. Grade control personnel direct which is ore and which is waste
4. **Hauling**: Dump trucks carry ore to the crusher/stockpile, waste to the dump
5. **Dewatering**: Continuous pumping to keep the pit dry, especially critical during rainy season

## 5.2 Underground Mining

Used when the deposit extends too deep for economic open pit mining.

**Common methods for gold in West Africa:**
- **Cut and Fill**: Mine one horizontal slice, backfill with waste rock or ceite, mine the next slice above. **Most common for narrow, high-grade orogenic veins**
- **Sub-Level Stoping (SLS)**: For steep, regular ore bodies. Drill and blast from different levels, ore falls to draw points below
- **Room and Pillar**: For flat, thick ore bodies

## 5.3 Artisanal & Small-Scale Mining (ASM)

ASM represents one of the oldest and most widespread forms of mining globally, employing an estimated 15–20 million people worldwide. In Africa alone, ASM supports the livelihoods of over 10 million miners and their families.

**Defining Characteristics:**
- Low mechanization — ranging from hand tools (picks, shovels, pans) to small excavators and basic wash plants
- Processing primarily by gravity methods (sluice boxes, panning, simple centrifugal concentrators)
- Labour-intensive, typically employing large numbers of local workers per operation
- Produces approximately 600–800 tonnes of gold globally per year, representing 15–20% of total world mine production
- Often operating in informal or semi-formal regulatory frameworks, with formalization efforts underway in many African countries

**Scale Categories:**
| Category | Workforce | Equipment | Annual Output |
|----------|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| Artisanal (hand mining) | 5–50 workers | Hand tools, gold pans, sluice boxes | 0.5–5 kg Au/year |
| Small-scale mechanized | 50–300 workers | Excavator, trommel, wash plant | 5–50 kg Au/year |
| Semi-industrial | 100–500+ workers | Multiple excavators, processing plant, generator | 50–500 kg Au/year |

**Key Equipment for Mechanized Alluvial Operations:**
- Excavator (20–36 tonne class) for digging alluvial gravel
- Trommel screen or vibrating screen to remove oversize material (>20 mm)
- Sluice boxes, jigs, or spiral concentrators for primary gravity concentration
- Shaking tables or centrifugal concentrators (e.g., Knelson, iCON) for final upgrading
- Borax smelting furnace to produce doré bars

**ASM and the Global Gold Supply Chain:**
ASM-produced gold enters the formal supply chain through licensed buying offices, government-approved trading houses, or — in many cases — informal channels. Efforts to formalize ASM supply chains include the LBMA Responsible Gold Guidance, the OECD Due Diligence Guidance, and national certification schemes in countries like the DRC (ITSCI), Colombia, and several West African nations.

**Key Challenges:**
- Environmental impact — mercury use (being replaced by borax smelting), deforestation, river siltation
- Occupational health and safety — unshored pits, dust exposure, chemical handling without PPE
- Regulatory complexity — overlapping claims, contested permits, evolving legal frameworks
- Community dynamics — ASM often predates formal exploration and establishes de facto land rights

## 5.4 Gold Processing — Key Methods

Gold processing transforms raw ore or alluvial material into refined gold. The choice of processing method depends on the ore type (free-milling vs. refractory), gold particle size (coarse vs. fine), grade, and scale of operation. Most operations use a combination of methods in sequence.

### Processing Decision Tree

| Ore Type | Gold Characteristics | Recommended Method | Expected Recovery |
|----------|---------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Alluvial gravel | Coarse, free gold (>100 μm) | Gravity separation | 70–90% |
| Oxidized rock ore | Free gold, moderate grade | Gravity + CIL/CIP | 90–96% |
| Fresh (sulfide) rock ore | Gold locked in sulfides | Flotation + CIL/CIP or roasting | 85–95% |
| Low-grade, large tonnage | Fine gold, oxide ore | Heap leaching | 50–75% |
| Refractory ore | Gold locked in arsenopyrite/pyrite | Oxidation (POX/BIOX/roasting) + CIL | 80–95% |
| Double-refractory | Gold locked in sulfides + carbonaceous (preg-robbing) | Ultra-fine grinding + oxidation + CIL | 70–90% |

---

### Gravity Separation

Exploits the density difference between gold (19.3 g/cm³) and waste minerals (~2.65 g/cm³). The **oldest and safest** gold recovery method, and the foundation of alluvial gold mining.

**How gravity separation works:** Gold is approximately 7× denser than the surrounding sand, gravel, and rock. When gold-bearing material is agitated in water, gold particles settle faster and concentrate at the bottom. Every gravity device exploits this principle in different ways.

| Equipment | How It Works | Recovery | Best For | Throughput |
|-----------|-------------|----------|----------|------------|
| **Gold Pan** | Manual swirling in water; tilt to wash away light material | 50–70% (operator-dependent) | Prospecting, sampling | 0.01 t/hr |
| **Sluice Box** | Inclined trough with riffles; gold settles behind riffles, lighter material washes away | 60–80% (coarse gold) | ASM, alluvial | 5–50 t/hr |
| **Jig** | Pulsating water stratifies particles by density in repeated cycles | 70–85% | Medium scale | 10–100 t/hr |
| **Spiral Concentrator** | Slurry flows down a helical trough; centrifugal + gravity separates heavy from light | 50–70% | Continuous operations, no power needed | 1–5 t/hr per spiral |
| **Shaking Table** | Asymmetric reciprocating motion on an inclined deck with riffles separates by density and size | >95% coarse gold | Final cleaning / upgrading of concentrates | 0.5–2 t/hr |
| **Centrifugal Concentrator** (Knelson/Falcon) | Enhanced gravity field (60–200G) forces fine gold into retention zones | >95% including fine gold (<50 μm) | Industrial and semi-industrial | 1–100 t/hr |
| **Trommel** | Rotating cylindrical screen; classifies material by size before gravity concentration | N/A (classification only) | Pre-screening alluvial gravel | 50–300 t/hr |

**Gravity Circuit Design — Typical Alluvial Operation:**
1. **Excavation:** Excavator digs alluvial gravel from the deposit
2. **Screening:** Trommel or vibrating screen removes oversize boulders and clay (>20 mm material rejected)
3. **Primary concentration:** Sluice box or jig recovers coarse gold (+200 μm)
4. **Secondary concentration:** Spiral or centrifugal concentrator recovers fine gold from sluice tailings
5. **Final upgrading:** Shaking table produces a high-grade concentrate (30–70% gold)
6. **Smelting:** Concentrate is smelted with borax to produce doré

**Key operating parameters:**
- **Feed rate** must match equipment capacity — overloading reduces recovery
- **Water flow** is critical: too little and light minerals don't wash away; too much and fine gold is lost
- **Gold liberation size** — gravity works best on free gold particles. If gold is locked inside sulfide minerals, gravity alone won't recover it
- **Clean-up frequency** — sluice boxes and jigs need regular clean-up (every 4–12 hours depending on grade) to prevent gold build-up reducing efficiency

---

### Cyanide Leaching (CIL / CIP)

The foundation of industrial gold processing. Used for approximately 80% of global gold production. Cyanide is the only reagent that can economically dissolve fine gold from large volumes of ore.

**The Chemistry:**
Gold dissolves in dilute cyanide solution in the presence of oxygen — a reaction known as Elsner's equation:
> 4Au + 8NaCN + O₂ + 2H₂O → 4Na[Au(CN)₂] + 4NaOH

This reaction is slow (24–72 hours for full dissolution) and requires careful control of pH (10–11, maintained with lime to prevent HCN gas generation), dissolved oxygen, and cyanide concentration.

**CIL (Carbon-in-Leach) Process — Step by Step:**
1. **Crushing:** Run-of-mine ore is crushed in jaw and cone crushers to <12 mm
2. **Grinding:** Ball mills or SAG mills grind the ore to ~75 μm (200 mesh) — a fine powder where ~80% of gold is liberated from the host rock
3. **Thickening:** Slurry is thickened to 40–50% solids to reduce water consumption
4. **Leaching:** Slurry is pumped through 6–10 large agitated tanks (each 500–2,000 m³) in series. Sodium cyanide (200–500 ppm) is added, and oxygen is sparged into the slurry
5. **Carbon adsorption:** Activated carbon (coconut shell, 6–12 mesh) is added directly into the leach tanks. The carbon adsorbs dissolved gold from solution — this is the "carbon-in-leach" step
6. **Carbon recovery:** Carbon is screened from the slurry using inter-stage screens and advanced counter-current to the slurry flow
7. **Elution (stripping):** Loaded carbon is treated with hot caustic cyanide solution (Zadra or AARL process) to strip gold back into solution
8. **Electrowinning:** Gold-bearing strip solution passes through electrolytic cells where gold is deposited onto steel wool cathodes
9. **Smelting:** Gold-loaded steel wool is smelted at 1,100°C with fluxes to produce doré bars (typically 85–95% gold)
10. **Carbon regeneration:** Stripped carbon is reactivated in a kiln at 700°C and recycled

**CIP (Carbon-in-Pulp) vs. CIL:**
- **CIP:** Leaching and carbon adsorption happen in separate stages — ore is first fully leached, then carbon is added. Better for slower-leaching ores
- **CIL:** Leaching and adsorption happen simultaneously. Faster, lower gold inventory in circuit. Preferred for most new plants

**Recovery:** 90–96% for free-milling (oxide) ores
**Capital cost:** $200–500M+ for a 2–5 Mtpa CIL plant
**Operating cost:** $15–30/t ore processed (excluding mining)
**Cyanide consumption:** 0.5–2.0 kg NaCN per tonne of ore

**Cyanide Safety:**
- Sodium cyanide (NaCN) is highly toxic — lethal dose is 1–3 mg/kg body weight
- Operations must comply with the International Cyanide Management Code (ICMC)
- At pH <9.5, cyanide converts to hydrogen cyanide gas (HCN) — extremely dangerous. This is why pH is maintained above 10 with lime
- Cyanide in tailings is destroyed by oxidation (SO₂/air process, Caro's acid, or hydrogen peroxide) before discharge to the tailings storage facility

---

### Flotation

Flotation is used when gold is finely disseminated within sulfide minerals (pyrite, arsenopyrite) and cannot be liberated by grinding alone. It is a pre-concentration step that produces a sulfide concentrate for further processing.

**How it works:**
1. Finely ground ore (~75 μm) is mixed with water and chemical reagents in flotation cells
2. **Collectors** (xanthates) make sulfide mineral surfaces hydrophobic (water-repelling)
3. **Frothers** (MIBC, pine oil) create a stable froth on the surface
4. Air is blown through the cell — sulfide minerals (with attached gold) attach to air bubbles and float to the surface
5. The froth (containing the concentrate) is skimmed off; the waste (tailings) sinks

**Typical results:** Flotation concentrates 90%+ of the gold into 5–15% of the original ore mass, producing a concentrate grading 20–100 g/t gold (compared to 1–5 g/t head grade). This concentrate is then processed by CIL, roasting, or pressure oxidation.

---

### Heap Leaching

A lower-cost alternative for low-grade (0.3–1.5 g/t), large-tonnage, oxidized ores. Heap leaching sacrifices recovery for dramatically lower capital costs.

**Process — Step by Step:**
1. **Crushing:** Ore is crushed to -19 to -25 mm (coarser than CIL grinding)
2. **Agglomeration:** Fine ore is mixed with cement (5–10 kg/t) to prevent blinding of the heap with fines
3. **Stacking:** Ore is stacked by truck or conveyor/stacker onto an impermeable HDPE liner, typically in 6–10 m lifts
4. **Irrigation:** Dilute cyanide solution (200–500 ppm NaCN) is applied via drip emitters at 8–12 L/hr/m²
5. **Percolation:** Solution percolates down through the heap over 60–120 days, dissolving gold
6. **Collection:** Gold-bearing "pregnant solution" drains to a collection pond at the base of the heap
7. **Gold recovery:** Pregnant solution is processed through activated carbon columns (carbon-in-column, CIC) or Merrill-Crowe zinc precipitation
8. **Recirculation:** Barren solution is re-fortified with cyanide and recirculated

**Recovery:** 50–75% (lower than CIL but at much lower capital cost)
**Capital cost:** $50–150M for a 5–20 Mtpa heap leach operation
**Operating cost:** $5–12/t ore (significantly lower than CIL)
**Best suited for:** Arid climates (rain dilutes solution), oxide ores (sulfide ores don't leach well), large deposits with low grades

**Advantages over CIL:**
- 50–70% lower capital cost
- Simpler operation — fewer moving parts, lower technical skill requirements
- Scalable — can start small and expand
- No grinding circuit (the single most expensive part of a CIL plant)

---

### Refractory Ore Treatment

Some gold ores are "refractory" — the gold is physically locked inside sulfide mineral crystals (mainly arsenopyrite and pyrite) and cannot be dissolved by cyanide without first breaking down the sulfide host. Refractory ores require an oxidation step before cyanide leaching.

| Method | How It Works | Capital Cost | Operating Cost | Best For |
|--------|-------------|-------------|----------------|----------|
| **Roasting** | Heat concentrate to 500–700°C in air; sulfides oxidize to iron oxide, releasing gold | High ($300M+) | Moderate | High-grade concentrates, established technology |
| **POX (Pressure Oxidation)** | Autoclave at 200°C, 3,000 kPa with oxygen; sulfides dissolve in sulfuric acid | Very high ($400M+) | High | Arsenopyrite-rich ores, best recovery |
| **BIOX (Biological Oxidation)** | Bacteria (Acidithiobacillus) eat sulfides over 4–6 days in stirred reactors | Moderate ($200M+) | Low | Lower-grade concentrates, lower CAPEX than POX |
| **Ultra-fine grinding** | Grind to <10 μm to physically liberate gold from sulfide crystals | Moderate | Moderate-high (energy) | Moderately refractory ores |

---

### Smelting: From Concentrate to Doré

Smelting is the final step at the mine site — converting gold concentrate into a semi-pure gold bar (doré) for shipment to a refinery.

**Borax Smelting** — the most important method for alluvial and small-scale operations:
1. Mix gravity concentrate (1–30% gold) with borax (Na₂B₄O₇) and soda ash (Na₂CO₃) as fluxes
2. Heat to 1,100–1,200°C in a graphite or clay crucible
3. Borax melts into a glassy slag that absorbs silicate and oxide impurities
4. Gold (being densest) sinks to the bottom of the crucible
5. Cool, break slag, extract gold button or bar

**Flux recipes vary by concentrate composition:**
| Concentrate Type | Borax | Soda Ash | Silica | Other |
|-----------------|-------|----------|--------|-------|
| Clean gravity concentrate (>30% Au) | 2:1 flux:concentrate | — | — | Minimal flux needed |
| Iron-rich concentrate | 3:1 | 10% | — | Add manganese dioxide as oxidant |
| Sulfide-rich concentrate | 3:1 | 15% | 10% | Add niter (KNO₃) to oxidize sulfides |
| Silica-rich concentrate | 2:1 | 20% | — | Extra soda ash to flux silica |

**Output purity:** 85–95% Au (doré), which must be sent to a refinery for further purification to 99.5%+ (London Good Delivery standard) or 99.99% (investment grade).

**Industrial smelting** at large mines uses induction furnaces (50–500 kg capacity) that can process large batches of electrowinning sludge or gravity concentrate in 2–4 hours. Doré bars are typically 20–30 kg and are shipped under armed escort to a licensed refinery.

**Borax smelting is the preferred alternative to mercury amalgamation** — non-toxic, higher recovery (90%+ vs mercury's 30–60%), and extremely low equipment cost ($100–1,000). The global movement to eliminate mercury from ASM (Minamata Convention) is driving rapid adoption of borax methods.

---

### Processing Method Comparison Summary

| Method | Capital Cost | Operating Cost | Recovery | Min. Grade | Scale |
|--------|-------------|----------------|----------|-----------|-------|
| Gravity only | $0.1–2M | $5–15/t | 60–85% | 0.5 g/t alluvial | ASM to small mine |
| Gravity + CIL | $200–500M | $20–40/t | 90–96% | 1.0 g/t rock | Industrial |
| Heap leach | $50–150M | $5–12/t | 50–75% | 0.3 g/t rock | Large, low-grade |

| Flotation + POX + CIL | $500M+ | $35–55/t | 85–95% | 2.0 g/t rock | Large, refractory |

## 5.6 Health, Safety & Security `[ALL]` `[OPS]`

Mining is one of the most hazardous industries globally. Every mining team member must understand and prioritize safety.

### Common Hazards in Gold Mining

| Hazard Category | Specific Risks | Prevention |
|----------------|----------------|------------|
| **Ground failure** | Pit wall collapse, underground roof fall, trench cave-in | Proper slope design, ground support, no entry to unsupported areas |
| **Mobile equipment** | Collision, rollover, struck-by incidents | Traffic management plan, spotters, seatbelts, training |
| **Drowning** | Flooded pits, water-filled trenches, river operations | Dewatering, barriers, life jackets for alluvial operations |
| **Falls** | Working at height on equipment, steep pit walls | Fall protection, ladders, access controls |
| **Heat stress** | West Africa temperatures regularly exceed 40°C | Hydration protocols, shade, work-rest cycles, early starts |
| **Dust** | Silicosis from prolonged quartz dust exposure | Wet drilling, dust suppression, respiratory protection |
| **Chemical** | Cyanide, fuel, mercury (in ASM areas) | SDS availability, training, PPE, proper storage, cyanide code |
| **Biological** | Malaria, waterborne diseases, snake/scorpion bites | Prophylaxis, treated nets, first aid kits, medical evacuation plan |
| **Explosive** | Misfired charges, improper storage | Licensed blasters only, strict procedures, magazine security |

### Minimum PPE Requirements

| Site Type | Required PPE |
|-----------|-------------|
| All mine sites | Hard hat, safety boots (steel toe), high-visibility vest, safety glasses |
| Drilling operations | + hearing protection, dust mask/respirator |
| Processing plant | + chemical-resistant gloves, face shield (near chemicals) |
| Alluvial/river operations | + life jacket, waterproof boots |
| Laboratory (fire assay) | + heat-resistant gloves, full face shield, leather apron |

### Emergency Response

Every mine site must have:
1. **Emergency response plan** — documented, trained, and rehearsed
2. **First aid** — trained first aiders on every shift, stocked first aid stations
3. **Medical evacuation** — defined evacuation route and vehicle, nearest hospital identified, satellite phone or reliable communication
4. **Fire response** — fire extinguishers at all fuel storage, processing, and camp areas
5. **Incident reporting** — all incidents (including near-misses) must be reported and investigated

### Security in West Africa

Mining operations in West Africa face specific security challenges:

- **Gold theft** — internal and external. Requires: gold room security, dual custody for all gold handling, CCTV, controlled access, random searches
- **Armed robbery** — particularly during gold transport. Requires: armed escort for gold movements, varied routes and schedules, secure vehicles
- **Community conflict** — disputes over land, employment, or environmental impact. Requires: proactive community engagement (see Section 7.4)
- **Artisanal miner encroachment** — illegal miners on licensed ground. Requires: boundary demarcation, regular patrols, community agreements, government liaison
- **Political instability** — particularly relevant in Mali and Guinea. Requires: evacuation plans, embassy registration, situation monitoring, cash reserves

**Best practice**: Zero tolerance for safety shortcuts. No production target justifies putting a team member at risk.

## 5.7 Water Management `[OPS]` `[LEGAL]`

Water is both essential for mining operations and a critical environmental concern. In West Africa, water management is complicated by extreme seasonal variation — months of drought followed by intense rainy seasons.

### Water Uses in Mining

| Operation | Water Use | Typical Consumption |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| Drilling (RC/DD) | Cooling, dust suppression, sample recovery | 5,000–20,000 L/day per rig |
| Processing (gravity) | Sluice boxes, jigs, wash plants | 50–200 m³/hour |
| Processing (CIL) | Slurry makeup, reagent mixing | 500–2,000 m³/hour |
| Dust suppression | Haul roads, stockpiles | Variable, 10,000–50,000 L/day |
| Camp and domestic | Drinking, cooking, sanitation | 100–200 L/person/day |

### Water Sources

- **Surface water** (rivers, streams) — most common in West Africa but seasonally variable and requires permits
- **Groundwater** (boreholes) — more reliable but may require dewatering permits and impact assessment
- **Pit dewatering** — water pumped from mine pits can often be reused in processing
- **Recycled process water** — modern operations aim to recycle 80–95% of process water

### Seasonal Challenges in West Africa

| Season | Challenge | Management |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| **Dry season** (Nov–Apr) | Water scarcity for processing; dust; reduced river flow | Water storage, boreholes, increased recycling |
| **Rainy season** (May–Oct) | Pit flooding, road washout, tailings dam overflow risk, increased malaria | Dewatering capacity, drainage infrastructure, road maintenance, stormwater diversions |
| **Transition periods** | Flash flooding risk, unstable pit walls from saturation | Monitoring, restricted access, slope stability assessment |

### Environmental Water Requirements

- **Baseline water quality monitoring** — must be established before mining starts
- **Discharge permits** — any water discharged must meet national standards (typically pH 6–9, TSS <50 mg/L, cyanide <0.2 mg/L)
- **Tailings water management** — zero-discharge is the best practice target; tailings supernatant must be recycled
- **Downstream impact assessment** — monitor water quality downstream of operations
- **Closure obligation** — water quality must be maintained long after mining ceases

**For alluvial operations:** Water management is the single biggest operational variable. Too little water stops processing; too much floods the site. Successful alluvial operations maintain a reliable water supply year-round through a combination of river abstraction, settling ponds, and recycling.

# Part 6: Mine Economics & Valuation `[FINANCE]` `[BD]` `[MGMT]`

## 6.1 Cost Structure

### Key Cost Metrics

| Metric | Definition | What It Includes |
|--------|-----------|------------------|
| **C1 Cash Cost** | Direct cost to produce one ounce of gold | Mining + Processing + Site G&A + Refining + Royalties — By-product credits |
| **AISC** (All-In Sustaining Cost) | Total cost to sustain production | C1 Cash Cost + Sustaining Capex + Corporate G&A + Sustaining Exploration |
| **AIC** (All-In Cost) | Everything including growth capital | AISC + Growth Capex + Non-sustaining Exploration |

### Typical Cost Ranges

| Category | West Africa Small Mine | Global Average |
|----------|----------------------|----------------|
| Mining cost (per tonne ore) | $2–8 | $5–15 |
| Processing cost (per tonne) | $3–8 (gravity only) / $15–35 (CIL) | $10–25 |
| Site G&A (per tonne) | $5–15 | $5–12 |
| **AISC (per ounce)** | **$800–1,400** | **~$1,400 (2025 median)** |

### Cost Curve Positioning

Alluvial operations in West Africa typically achieve AISC of $800–1,200/oz, placing them in the **lower quartile** of the global cost curve. This is a significant competitive advantage: at any gold price, lower-cost producers survive longer and generate more margin.

## 6.2 Revenue Model

**Revenue = Gold produced (oz) × Realized gold price ($/oz)**

Or more precisely:

**Revenue = Ore processed (t) × Head grade (g/t) × Recovery (%) × Gold price ($/oz) ÷ 31.1035**

### Example (West Africa Alluvial Operation, monthly)

```
Alluvial material processed:   ~60 m³/day × 30 days = 1,800 m³
Average grade:                 ~1.2 g/m³
Recovery rate:                 ~85%
Gold produced:                 1,800 × 1.2 × 0.85 = 1,836 g = 59 oz
Gold price:                    $2,950/oz
Gross revenue:                 ~$174,000/month

(Note: A typical small-scale alluvial operation may produce ~1-3 kg/day = ~30-90 kg/month)
```

## 6.3 Key Financial Metrics `[FINANCE]`

### Net Present Value (NPV)
- Discounts all future cash flows of a mining project to today's value
- Discount rate: typically 5–8% (real, after-tax)
- **The gold standard of mining project evaluation**
- NPV > 0 means the project creates value

### Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
- The discount rate at which NPV = 0
- Minimum acceptable IRR for mining: 15–25% (higher for riskier jurisdictions)
- West Africa projects typically require higher IRR hurdles due to political risk

### Payback Period
- Time to recover initial investment from operating cash flows
- Banks typically want payback within 3–5 years
- Shorter payback = lower risk

## 6.4 Valuation Methods `[BD]` `[FINANCE]`

| Method | When to Use | How It Works |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| **DCF / NPV** | Development to production stage | Model all future cash flows, discount to present value. Requires feasibility-level data |
| **EV/Resource oz** | Exploration to development stage | Enterprise Value ÷ Total resource ounces. Quick comparability metric |
| **Comparable Transactions** | Any stage | What did similar projects trade for? $/oz in ground, $/ha |
| **P/NAV Multiple** | Publicly traded companies | Share price ÷ Net Asset Value per share |

### Typical Valuation Ranges by Stage

| Stage | EV per Resource Ounce | P/NAV Multiple |
|-------|----------------------|----------------|
| Grassroots exploration | $5–20/oz | — |
| Advanced exploration (resource defined) | $20–50/oz (Inferred), $50–150/oz (Indicated) | 0.1–0.3x |
| Development (feasibility complete) | $100–250/oz | 0.3–0.7x |
| Production | $150–400/oz | 0.8–1.2x |

**Key insight for BD**: Value increases dramatically at each de-risking milestone. A discovery confirmed by drilling is worth many times more than an untested anomaly. A JORC resource is worth many times more than drill intercepts. This is the fundamental value creation mechanism in exploration.

# Part 7: Legal & Regulatory Framework `[LEGAL]` `[MGMT]`

## 7.1 Mining Licence Types

In most African jurisdictions, minerals belong to the state. Companies must obtain licences to explore and mine.

### Typical Licence Progression

| Licence Type | Activities Allowed | Area | Duration | Renewal |
|-------------|-------------------|------|----------|---------|
| **Reconnaissance Licence** | Non-invasive surveys (remote sensing, regional geochemistry) | Large (thousands of km²) | 1–2 years | Limited |
| **Exploration Permit (EP)** | Ground surveys, sampling, drilling | Medium (100–1,000 km²) | 2–4 years | 1–2 renewals, with mandatory relinquishment |
| **Mining Permit / Exploitation Permit** | Full-scale mining and processing | Small (defined by resource) | 15–25 years | Renewable |

### Typical Government Requirements
- **Work commitments**: Minimum exploration expenditure per year
- **Reporting**: Annual technical reports, production data, employment data
- **Environmental**: Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) / Environmental and Social Impact Assessment (ESIA) before mining
- **Local content**: Local hiring preferences, local procurement requirements
- **Area relinquishment**: Must give back a percentage of exploration area at each renewal (typically 25–50%)

## 7.2 Royalty Structures `[LEGAL]` `[FINANCE]`

A royalty is a payment to the government (or licence holder) based on production.

| Type | Basis | Common Rates |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **Ad Valorem Royalty** | Percentage of gross revenue (gold sold × price) | 3–6% |
| **NSR (Net Smelter Return)** | Percentage of revenue after deducting smelting/refining costs | 1–5% |
| **NPI (Net Profit Interest)** | Percentage of net profit after deducting all costs | 5–20% |
| **GRR (Gross Revenue Royalty)** | Same as ad valorem | 3–6% |
| **Government Free Carry** | Government gets a free equity stake in the mining company | 10–20% |

### Country-Specific Rates

| Country | Royalty Rate | Government Free Carry | Corporate Tax |
|---------|-------------|----------------------|---------------|
| Mali | 3% on revenue | 10% free + option for additional 10% | 25% |
| Guinea | 5% on revenue | 15% free | 25% |
| Cameroon | 4% on revenue | 10% free | 30% |
| CDI (Côte d'Ivoire) | 3–5% on revenue (sliding scale) | 10% free | 25% |
| Namibia | Variable (typically 3–5%) | — | 32% (mining) |
| Botswana | 3% on gross market value | — | 22% |

## 7.3 Joint Venture & Earn-in Agreements `[LEGAL]` `[BD]`

These are the most common deal structures in exploration-stage mining.

### Earn-in / Option Agreement
- **Company A** (the "farmer") holds a mining licence
- **Company B** (the "farmee") wants to earn an interest
- Company B commits to spend $X on exploration over Y years
- Upon meeting the expenditure requirement, Company B earns a specified percentage interest (e.g., 51%, 70%, 80%)
- **Key terms to negotiate**: earn-in expenditure amount, timeline, what counts as "expenditure", free-carry provisions, dilution mechanics, operatorship

### Key Contractual Terms `[LEGAL]`

| Term | Definition | Why It Matters |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| **NSR Royalty** | Ongoing royalty on production payable to the original licence holder | Can significantly reduce project economics. Negotiate caps or buyback provisions |
| **Dilution** | What happens if a party doesn't fund their share of future costs | Must be defined precisely. Standard formula: new % = old % × (old % contribution / total cost) |
| **Change of Control** | What happens if a party is acquired by a third party | Can trigger consent requirements or pre-emptive rights |
| **Area of Interest** | Geographic zone around the licence where both parties share discoveries | Defines the exclusion zone |
| **Operatorship** | Who manages day-to-day operations | The operator has significant control — ensure clear reporting and approval thresholds |
| **Anti-dilution** | Protection against being diluted below a certain threshold | Typically below 10% interest, party converts to a royalty |
| **Tail Period** | Period after termination where the exiting party retains rights | Can create long-term obligations — negotiate carefully |

## 7.4 Environmental & Social Requirements `[LEGAL]`

### Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA / ESIA)

Required before any mining permit is granted. Typically includes:
- Baseline environmental studies (water, air, soil, biodiversity) — 12–24 months of data
- Impact assessment for proposed operations
- Mitigation and management plans
- Closure and rehabilitation plan
- Public consultation and disclosure
- Independent review

### Community Relations

**Social License to Operate** may be the most important non-technical success factor for mining in West Africa.

Key principles:
1. **Early and continuous communication** with village chiefs and local government
2. **Prioritize local hiring** — the most effective social licence tool
3. **Fair land compensation** with independent assessors and documented processes
4. **Community investment**: allocate 1–2% of revenue to community development (schools, clinics, wells, roads)
5. **Establish a grievance mechanism**: formal process for community complaints and responses

### Mine Closure `[LEGAL]`

Every mining permit requires a closure plan. This is an increasingly important legal and financial obligation:
- Rehabilitation of disturbed land
- Safe closure of tailings facilities
- Ongoing water monitoring (potentially decades)
- Financial assurance / closure bond required in most jurisdictions

## 7.5 Country Notes

### Côte d'Ivoire (CDI)
- Mining Code: 2014, amended 2023
- SARL is the recommended corporate structure for mining entities
- Exploration permits valid 4 years, renewable twice
- Government automatically takes 10% free carry at mining stage
- Strong French-language legal system

### Namibia

- **Note**: Political instability remains a significant risk factor

## 8.2 LBMA Good Delivery Standard

The **London Bullion Market Association (LBMA)** sets the global standard for gold bullion:

| Specification | Requirement |
|---------------|-------------|
| Minimum fineness | 995.0 (99.5% pure) |
| Weight range | 350–430 troy ounces (~10.9–13.4 kg) |
| Marks required | Serial number, refiner's stamp, fineness, year |
| Assay | Independent assay confirming fineness |

**Only ~70 refineries worldwide hold LBMA Good Delivery accreditation.** Gold refined by these facilities is accepted universally — in banks, exchanges, and central bank reserves. Non-LBMA gold trades at a discount.

## 8.3 Dubai as a Gold Trading Hub

Dubai (specifically DMCC — Dubai Multi Commodities Centre) is the world's **second-largest physical gold trading hub** after Switzerland.

**Key facts (2024):**
- Precious metals trade volume: AED 625 billion (~USD 170 billion)
- 2,500+ precious metals member companies in DMCC
- Multiple LBMA-accredited refineries (Kaloti, Emirates Gold, Al Etihad)
- Strategic geographic position between African supply and Asian demand

**For small-scale producers:** Small-scale producers (<1 tonne/year) typically work with mid-size Dubai traders and non-LBMA refineries initially, then graduate to direct LBMA refinery relationships as compliance documentation matures.

## 8.4 Gold Pricing

| Benchmark | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **LBMA Gold Price** | Set twice daily (10:30 AM and 3:00 PM London time) by ICE Benchmark Administration. The global reference price |
| **COMEX Futures** | Most actively traded gold futures contract (CME Group). Tracks closely with LBMA price |
| **Spot Price** | Real-time traded price on global markets |

**Doré pricing**: Doré is typically sold at the LBMA price minus:
- Refining charges ($1–5/oz)
- Assay/transport/insurance ($1–3/oz)
- A treatment charge or discount for the specific doré's purity

## 8.5 Supply Chain Compliance `[LEGAL]`

### LBMA Responsible Gold Guidance (RGG)
- Requires all LBMA-accredited refineries to conduct due diligence on their gold supply chains
- Based on the **OECD Five-Step Due Diligence Framework**
- Version 10 (2025–2026) requires public disclosure of suppliers from "red flag" regions

### OECD Five-Step Framework
1. Establish strong company management systems
2. Identify and assess risks in the supply chain
3. Design and implement a strategy to respond to identified risks
4. Carry out independent third-party audit
5. Report on supply chain due diligence

### What Small-Scale Producers Need
- Complete **Chain of Custody** documentation (from mine to buyer)
- **Know Your Customer (KYC)** documentation for all counterparties
- **Certificate of Origin** for all gold exports
- Export permits from the host country
- Internal compliance policy aligned with OECD framework

## 9.6 Technology & Innovation in Mining `[MGMT]` `[BD]`

The mining industry is undergoing significant technological transformation. Understanding these trends is important for any exploration company's positioning and competitive advantage.

### Drone Technology

| Application | How It Works | Cost Saving vs Traditional |
|------------|-------------|--------------------------|
| **Topographic survey** | Drone-mounted LiDAR or photogrammetry creates detailed 3D terrain models | 70–90% cheaper than ground survey for same area |
| **Pit volume measurement** | Weekly drone flights to track material movement and reconcile with production data | Replaces manual survey teams |
| **Exploration mapping** | High-resolution orthophotos for geological mapping in areas with poor satellite coverage | Fills gap between satellite (30m) and field mapping |
| **Stockpile measurement** | Accurate volume calculations of ore and waste stockpiles | Replaces estimate-based inventory |
| **Environmental monitoring** | Track rehabilitation progress, detect illegal mining, monitor water bodies | Covers large areas quickly |
| **Security** | Perimeter surveillance, monitoring artisanal miner activity | Supplements ground patrols |

**Opportunity for junior explorers**: Drone surveys for topographic mapping and pit planning at alluvial sites can be done with consumer-grade drones ($2–5K) and free photogrammetry software (OpenDroneMap), delivering professional-grade results at minimal cost.

### Portable XRF Analysers

Handheld X-ray fluorescence (XRF) analysers provide instant multi-element geochemistry in the field:
- **Analysis time**: 30–120 seconds per sample
- **Elements detected**: 30+ elements simultaneously (including gold pathfinder elements: As, Cu, Sb, Bi, W)
- **Accuracy**: Semi-quantitative (±10–20% for most elements); not a substitute for fire assay but excellent for screening
- **Cost**: $25–50K per unit
- **Key use**: Real-time decision-making during drilling — determine if an interval is mineralized before waiting weeks for lab results

**Limitation**: XRF cannot directly detect gold at exploration-relevant grades (<10 g/t) because gold's detection limit by XRF is typically 5–10 ppm. Instead, geologists use **pathfinder elements** (arsenic, antimony, bismuth) that are detectable by XRF and commonly associated with orogenic gold.

### Data Management & Digital Geology

Modern exploration generates enormous amounts of data. Key tools:

| Tool Category | Examples | Purpose |
|--------------|---------|---------|
| **GIS** | QGIS (free), ArcGIS Pro | Spatial data integration, map production, multi-layer analysis |
| **3D Geological Modelling** | Leapfrog Geo, Micromine | Build 3D geological models from drill data, generate resource estimates |
| **Database** | acQuire, Geobank, DataShed | Centralized storage of all exploration data (drill holes, samples, assays) |
| **Remote Sensing** | ENVI, Google Earth Engine (free) | Satellite image processing, spectral analysis, change detection |
| **Project Management** | Geotrack, custom dashboards | Track exploration expenditure, licence commitments, reporting deadlines |

**Best practice:** Building a centralized geological database early is critical. As operations scale across multiple countries, the ability to query, compare, and analyze data across all projects becomes a major competitive advantage.

### Emerging Technologies to Watch

| Technology | Maturity | Potential Impact |
|-----------|---------|-----------------|
| **Hyperspectral core scanning** | Commercial | Automated mineral identification from drill core; 10x faster than manual logging |
| **Ambient noise tomography** | Early commercial | Passive seismic imaging of subsurface structures without active sources |
| **Distributed Acoustic Sensing (DAS)** | Research | Converts fibre optic cables into thousands of seismic sensors |
| **Autonomous drilling** | Commercial (surface) | Removes operators from hazardous environments; 24/7 operation |

# Part 10: Glossary `[ALL]`

## A

## B

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Bankable Feasibility Study (BFS)** | The most detailed level of engineering and economic study; sufficient for bank financing |

## C

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|

| **CIL** (Carbon-in-Leach) | Industrial gold processing method where activated carbon adsorbs dissolved gold from a cyanide leach solution |
| **CIP** (Carbon-in-Pulp) | Similar to CIL but leaching occurs separately before carbon adsorption |
| **Competent Person (CP)** | A qualified professional authorized to sign mineral resource/reserve reports under JORC |

| Term | Definition |

| **DD** (Diamond Drilling) | Drilling that produces a cylindrical rock core; highest quality samples |
| **Dilution** | (Mining) Mixing of waste rock with ore, reducing head grade. (Corporate) Reduction in ownership percentage due to new shares issued |
| **Doré** | Semi-pure gold-silver alloy bar produced at the mine site; typically 80–95% gold |

## E

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Earn-in** | Agreement where one party earns a stake in a mining project by funding exploration expenditure |
| **EIA / ESIA** | Environmental (and Social) Impact Assessment; required before mining permits are granted |

## F

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Feasibility Study (FS)** | Engineering study determining whether a project is technically and economically viable |
| **Fire Assay** | Standard laboratory method for determining gold content; involves fusion at 1,100°C |
| **Free Carry** | Government's free equity interest in a mining company (typically 10–20%) |
| **Free Gold** | Gold that can be recovered by gravity methods (not locked inside sulfide minerals) |

## G

| Term | Definition |

| **g/t** (grams per tonne) | Standard unit of gold grade for hard rock deposits |
| **g/m³** (grams per cubic metre) | Standard unit of gold grade for alluvial deposits |
| **Gravity Separation** | Processing method that exploits density differences to separate gold from waste |
| **Greenstone Belt** | A belt of metamorphosed volcanic-sedimentary rocks; hosts major gold deposits worldwide |

## H–I

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Head Grade** | The average gold grade of ore fed into the processing plant |
| **Heap Leach** | Low-cost processing method where cyanide solution percolates through a pile of crushed ore |
| **Hydrothermal** | Relating to hot water; hydrothermal fluids are the main agents that transport and deposit gold |
| **Indicated Resource** | Mineral resource with moderate confidence; suitable for PFS |
| **Inferred Resource** | Mineral resource with low confidence; suitable only for PEA |
| **IP** (Induced Polarization) | Geophysical method that detects sulfide minerals in the ground |
| **IRR** (Internal Rate of Return) | The discount rate at which a project's NPV equals zero; minimum threshold typically 15–25% |

## J–L

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **JORC** | Joint Ore Reserves Committee; Australian standard for reporting mineral resources and reserves |
| **JV** (Joint Venture) | Partnership between two or more parties to develop a mining project |
| **Laterite** | Deep tropical weathering product; covers much of West Africa's bedrock to 20–80 m depth |
| **LBMA** | London Bullion Market Association; sets the global standard for gold bullion |

## M–N

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Measured Resource** | Mineral resource with high confidence; suitable for FS and reserve conversion |
| **Moz** (Million ounces) | Standard unit for describing deposit size |
| **NI 43-101** | Canadian standard for reporting mineral resources and reserves |
| **NPV** (Net Present Value) | Present value of all future cash flows minus initial investment |
| **NSR** (Net Smelter Return) | Royalty based on revenue after deducting smelting and refining costs |
| **Nugget Effect** | Statistical challenge caused by gold's extremely uneven distribution in deposits |

## O–P

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Opex** (Operating Expenditure) | Ongoing costs of running a mine (labour, fuel, reagents, etc.) |
| **Orogenic** | Relating to mountain-building processes; orogenic gold deposits form along deep fault zones during these events |
| **Oxide / Oxidized** | Near-surface rock that has been weathered; oxide gold ore is typically free-milling (easy to process) |
| **Pay Zone / Pay Streak** | The layer within an alluvial deposit that contains economic gold grades |
| **PEA** (Preliminary Economic Assessment) | Initial economic study; can use Inferred Resources |
| **PFS** (Pre-Feasibility Study) | Intermediate study; requires at least Indicated Resources |
| **Placer** | A deposit of sand or gravel containing valuable minerals (especially gold) concentrated by gravity |
| **Probable Reserve** | Economically mineable part of an Indicated Resource |
| **Proven Reserve** | Economically mineable part of a Measured Resource |

## Q–R

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|

| **QP** (Qualified Person) | Canadian equivalent of JORC's Competent Person |
| **RC** (Reverse Circulation) | Drilling method that produces rock chips; main workhorse for West Africa exploration |
| **Recovery Rate** | Percentage of gold in the ore that is actually extracted by the processing plant |
| **Refractory Gold** | Gold locked inside sulfide minerals; cannot be extracted by simple cyanidation without pre-treatment |
| **Resource** | See Mineral Resource |
| **Reserve** | See Mineral Reserve |
| **Royalty** | Payment to government or licence holder based on production |

## S–T

| Term | Definition |

| **Shear Zone** | A zone of intense deformation in the earth's crust; major shear zones are pathways for gold-bearing fluids |
| **Sluice Box** | Simple gravity device — an inclined trough with riffles that traps gold |
| **Streaming** | Financing arrangement where investor pays upfront for the right to buy a percentage of future production at a fixed discount |
| **Strip Ratio** | Ratio of waste rock to ore (e.g., 5:1 means 5 tonnes waste removed for every 1 tonne ore) |
| **Supergene** | Near-surface processes (weathering, groundwater) that can enrich gold deposits |
| **Tailings** | Waste material remaining after ore has been processed; stored in tailings storage facilities (TSF) |
| **Troy Ounce** | The standard unit for precious metals; 31.1035 grams |
